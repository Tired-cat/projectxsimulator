# Reasoning Board — User Guide

## What Is It?

The Reasoning Board is a structured thinking tool. It forces you to explain *why* you made a budget decision, not just *what* you changed. Think of it as writing the memo you would send your boss justifying your marketing spend.

It has four quadrants in a specific order:

```
┌─────────────────────┬─────────────────────┐
│  1. DESCRIPTIVE     │  2. DIAGNOSTIC      │
│  What happened?     │  Why did it happen? │
│                     │                     │
│  Facts and          │  Root cause         │
│  observations       │  analysis           │
├─────────────────────┼─────────────────────┤
│  3. PRESCRIPTIVE    │  4. PREDICTIVE      │
│  What did you do?   │  What do you        │
│                     │  expect next?       │
│  Your decision      │  Your forecast      │
└─────────────────────┴─────────────────────┘
```

---

## Step-by-Step: How to Use It

### Step 1 — Explore the data first
Before touching the Reasoning Board, spend time in the simulation:

1. Open **My Decisions** tab
2. Drag the bars to try different budget splits
3. Switch tabs: Budget → Views → Revenue → Profit. Notice that the channel with the most *views* is NOT the top *revenue* earner
4. Scroll down to the **Product Mix** chart. This shows you *what* each channel actually sells

Do not start filling the board until you have a genuine observation you want to explain.

---

### Step 2 — Enter evidence mode
On the **Channel Performance** chart or the **Product Mix** chart, click the **Reason** button.

This turns the bars into draggable evidence items. Each bar becomes a chip you can pick up and drop onto the board.

> **Common mistake:** Students try to drag bars before clicking Reason. Nothing will happen unless Reason mode is active.

---

### Step 3 — Fill Descriptive first

**Question: What did you actually observe in the data?**

Drag an evidence bar onto the **Descriptive** quadrant. This should be a factual observation — something you can read directly off the chart.

Good Descriptive evidence:
- "TikTok Views: 18,000" — high traffic
- "Newspaper Revenue: $40,880" — high revenue despite low spend
- "Facebook Profit: +$18,420" — strong net return

Bad Descriptive evidence:
- Your decision ("I moved budget to Newspaper") — that belongs in Prescriptive
- A prediction — that belongs in Predictive

You can add more than one chip. If two pieces of data together tell a clearer story (e.g., TikTok has high views but low revenue), drag both.

**Contextualising:** Once a chip is on the board, you can drag a *second* chip and drop it directly *onto the first chip* to attach it as supporting evidence. This is useful for contrast ("TikTok Views: 18,000 — supported by TikTok Revenue: $1,330").

---

### Step 4 — Fill Diagnostic

**Question: Why is that happening?**

This is your explanation. You are not just re-stating the data — you are saying what is *causing* it.

Good Diagnostic evidence:
- A Product Mix chip showing TikTok's conversion breakdown (mostly $10 bottles)
- A Revenue chip showing why Newspaper earns more per click (high chair conversion)

Good Diagnostic thinking (in your own words):
> "TikTok generates volume but almost no chair sales because its audience doesn't buy $500 chairs. Newspaper readers are older and more likely to purchase premium furniture."

This kind of thinking is what the **"Add your interpretation"** field on each chip is for. Type your reasoning there — it gets incorporated into your narrative automatically.

> **Common mistake:** Copying the same evidence from Descriptive into Diagnostic. That is not diagnosis, it is repetition. The validation system will warn you.

---

### Step 5 — Fill Prescriptive

**Question: What action did you take?**

This is your decision. Which channel did you move budget to or from? Why?

Good Prescriptive evidence:
- A delta chip (▲ or ▼) showing a spend change you made
- A budget chip showing your new allocation

Good Prescriptive thinking:
> "I moved $7,000 from TikTok to Newspaper because Newspaper's revenue-per-dollar is ~40x better."

> **Important:** Your prescriptive action should logically address what you diagnosed. If you diagnosed that TikTok has low chair conversion, your prescription should not be "increase TikTok spend." The validation checks this.

---

### Step 6 — Fill Predictive

**Question: What result do you expect from your decision?**

This is your forecast. What should happen to revenue, profit, or views after your change?

Good Predictive evidence:
- A Revenue chip after your budget change (comparing to the Descriptive chip)
- A Profit chip showing the expected improvement
- A delta chip showing the revenue gain

Good Predictive thinking:
> "By shifting $8,000 to Newspaper, I expect total revenue to increase by roughly $120,000 because each additional dollar on Newspaper generates ~$40 in revenue."

---

### Step 7 — Check the Reasoning Chain

The banner at the top of the board will tell you if your chain is valid:

- **Green "Reasoning chain complete"** — all four quadrants have evidence and the chain is logically consistent
- **Amber "Reasoning chain needs fixes"** — click "View checks" to see what is wrong

Common validation errors and how to fix them:
| Error | Fix |
|-------|-----|
| `diagnostic is empty` | Add at least one chip to Diagnostic |
| `prescriptive has evidence before diagnostic` | Fill the quadrants in order |
| `Prescriptive actions do not clearly address diagnosed channels` | Your prescription should fix what you diagnosed — use evidence from the same channels |
| `The same evidence is reused across most blocks` | Use different data points in each quadrant, not the same chip everywhere |

---

### Step 8 — Read your Reasoning Story

Scroll below the four quadrants. You will see two sections:

**AT A GLANCE** — A bullet-point summary of each quadrant's data with a one-sentence interpretation.

**MY FULL REASONING STORY** — A flowing paragraph that stitches all four quadrants together into a narrative. Colour-coded by quadrant (blue for Descriptive, orange for Diagnostic, green for Prescriptive, purple for Predictive).

If the story sounds hollow or generic, it means your chip annotations are empty. Click the italic `+ Add your interpretation...` text on each chip and type what *you* think it means. That text flows directly into the story.

---

### Step 9 — Write your Key Insight

Below the story is an **"In Your Own Words"** text box.

This is where you summarise your single most important finding in plain language. Write 2-3 sentences. Example:

> "The simulation's biggest surprise was Newspaper. Despite tiny traffic, its 20% chair conversion rate means each click is worth $100 in revenue — compared to TikTok's $0.08 per click. Not all audience attention is equally valuable."

This box feeds directly into your AI feedback report.

---

### Step 10 — Get Feedback

Only when you have filled all four quadrants and written your key insight should you click **Get Feedback**.

**You only get this once.** The AI will read your full board, your annotations, and your key insight. Make sure your reasoning is complete and genuine before clicking — refine your thinking first, then use the feedback to learn from it.

---

## Quick Reference

| Quadrant | Question | What to drag | What NOT to do |
|----------|----------|--------------|----------------|
| Descriptive | What happened? | Facts from the chart as-is | Decisions or predictions |
| Diagnostic | Why did it happen? | Product mix, conversion context | Copy from Descriptive |
| Prescriptive | What did you do? | Spend changes (▲▼ delta chips) | Actions unrelated to your diagnosis |
| Predictive | What do you expect? | Revenue/profit after your change | Guesses with no logical basis |

---

## Tips

- **Use delta chips (▲▼)** — When in Compare Before/After mode, the bar chart shows delta indicators. These are the most useful chips for Prescriptive and Predictive because they directly show changes.
- **Contextualise, don't duplicate** — Instead of adding the same chip to multiple quadrants, drag it onto an existing chip to attach it as context.
- **Add annotations** — The narrative is auto-generated from your data. Your actual thinking only appears if you write it in the chip annotation field.
- **Use split view** — Drag the Reasoning Board tab to the right side of the screen to see the chart and the board at the same time. This makes dragging evidence much easier.
